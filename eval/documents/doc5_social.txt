Social Media's Impact on Adolescent Mental Health

Adolescent mental health has declined significantly since 2010, coinciding with smartphone and social media proliferation. Rates of anxiety, depression, and self-harm have increased, particularly among teenage girls.

Proposed mechanisms include social comparison, cyberbullying, sleep disruption from late-night scrolling, and displacement of in-person social interaction. Instagram's own internal research found its platform made body image issues worse for one in three teen girls.

However, causality remains debated. Some researchers argue pre-existing vulnerabilities drive both social media use and mental health issues, rather than social media causing problems. Cross-national studies show inconsistent correlations, suggesting cultural context matters.

Potential benefits include community-building for marginalized youth, mental health resource access, and creative expression. LGBTQ+ teens report social media as vital for finding support unavailable offline.

Policy responses range from age verification and parental controls to algorithmic transparency requirements. France banned smartphones in schools, while Australia proposed social media age minimums of 16. Critics warn such measures may drive teens to less moderated platforms.

Balanced approaches emphasize digital literacy education, platform design changes to reduce addictive features, and supporting parents in setting healthy technology boundaries rather than outright bans.
